abstract: |
  Fluid transport in biological networks is often governed by scaling laws that emerge from minimizing total energetic cost. Murray’s law, originally derived for vascular systems and now widely applied to advection-dominated networks such as mammalian arteries and plant xylem, provides one such principle. However, preliminary studies have suggested that it does not hold in insect tracheal networks, which conduct respiratory gas exchange directly between atmospheric air and tissues without a circulatory intermediary. Transport in these networks is mixed rather than purely advective, two-way rather than closed-loop, and spans continuum, slip-flow, and Knudsen regimes across their wide range of diameters. Here we develop a Murray-style scaling law tailored to insect tracheal networks by minimizing an energetic cost functional that includes both advective and diffusive transport terms, a metabolic maintenance term proportional to tracheal lumen volume or CO2 exchange surface area, and corrections for microscale phenomena such as hydrodynamic slip and rarefaction. The formulation incorporates the inherently two-way nature of insect respiration through a tidal transport model, in which forward and reverse phases of gas exchange occur sequentially in time. In this framework, oxygen uptake during the forward phase is dominated by diffusion, while carbon dioxide removal during the reverse phase is dominated by advection, reflecting the asymmetric transport mechanisms observed during cyclic spiracular opening, tracheal compression, and abdominal pumping. These alternating phases are represented through duty-cycle-weighted contributions to the total energetic cost, yielding an effective optimization principle for periodically driven respiratory networks. The resulting model predicts a continuum of branching exponents between 1 and 3, consistent with experimentally measured values across a number of insect species. These exponents reflect the dominant local transport regime, and therefore provide a useful instrument in the insect respiratory physiology experimental toolbox. Because direct measurements of intratracheal velocity and pressure remain challenging, particularly during locomotion, local scaling exponents offer a means of identifying whether advection, diffusion, or mixed transport governs a given region of the tracheal system. However, solving for these exponents from measured radii requires addressing a nonlinear, numerically ill-conditioned inverse problem. We quantify this conditioning and present an approach for determining which junctions yield reliable exponent estimates. Finally, we examine additional geometric and dynamic features of insect tracheae that modify effective transport. Computational analyses of real tracheal segments show that taenidial windings, curvature, and noncircular cross-sections generate helical secondary flows and enhance peak velocities relative to equivalent straight circular tubes. At small diameters, Holweck-like molecular-drag effects further amplify transport by reducing effective viscosity in the slip-Knudsen regime. Under rhythmic compression during locomotion or flight, plate-like collapse of large compliant tracheae can transiently steepen resistance scaling and locally elevate effective exponents. This framework unifies geometry, metabolic upkeep, and mixed advective-diffusive physics under a single power-minimization principle. It offers a predictive basis for interpreting respiratory network architecture across species and ecological contexts, identifying dominant local transport regimes, and linking structural variation to physiological performance under varying environmental oxygen conditions and time-varying respiratory demands. The framework may be extended to analyze tidal transport in other biological networks like the human tracheal system.
